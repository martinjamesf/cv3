Leadership Style

In leadership, success is rarely about simply hiring the most talented individuals. Instead, it’s about understanding people, developing their strengths, and placing them in the right environments to thrive. A strong leader must recognize the unique personality and working styles of their team members, help them develop into adaptable and multi-skilled contributors, and strategically place them in the right situations to optimize outcomes.

Many leaders fail because they adopt a one-size-fits-all approach, assuming that what works for one employee will work for all. This oversight leads to miscommunication, stagnation, and underutilized potential. True leadership involves recognizing the complexity of human behavior and tailoring management approaches accordingly. By leveraging the right frameworks, leaders can ensure that each team member is set up for success.

This article explores three critical frameworks that, when combined, provide a comprehensive leadership strategy: understanding personality types using Surrounded by Idiots by Thomas Erikson, fostering multi-disciplinary growth through the PI-shaped and Polygon-shaped professional models, and placing individuals in the right environments using the Cynefin Framework by Dave Snowden.

Key Frameworks

  • Surrounded by Idiots by Thomas Erikson – Categorizes personalities to enhance communication and collaboration.
  • PI-shaped or Polygon-shaped professionals – Helps individuals develop broad and deep skill sets.
  • Cynefin Framework by Dave Snowden – Ensures individuals operate in the right environments to drive success.

Each of these frameworks plays a crucial role in talent management. Erikson’s personality model helps leaders understand the motivations and communication styles of different individuals. The PI- and Polygon-shaped professional models guide professional development strategies to ensure adaptability. Finally, the Cynefin Framework provides a methodology for placing talent where they will be most effective.

When combined, these three approaches create a robust, people-centric leadership strategy that enables organizations to maximize performance, engagement, and innovation.

Reading People: The Power of Erikson’s Color Model

Thomas Erikson’s Surrounded by Idiots categorizes people into four colors:

  • Red (Dominant, direct, results-driven): Reds thrive on challenge and competition. They are goal-oriented, decisive, and efficient, making them ideal for leadership positions or roles that require quick decision-making. However, their direct nature can sometimes be perceived as aggressive or insensitive.
  • Yellow (Creative, enthusiastic, social): Yellows are the energy of a team. They bring innovation, charisma, and optimism, making them well-suited for brainstorming sessions, creative projects, and roles that require public engagement. However, their tendency to get easily distracted means they may require structure and accountability.
  • Green (Supportive, reliable, patient): Greens are team players who value stability and harmony. They excel in roles that require consistency and relationship-building. While they may avoid confrontation, they are essential for maintaining cohesion within a group.
  • Blue (Analytical, detail-oriented, precise): Blues focus on accuracy, logic, and planning. They are excellent in technical and data-driven roles where attention to detail is paramount. However, their preference for perfection can sometimes lead to over-analysis and hesitation in fast-moving environments.

Recognizing these personality types enables leaders to communicate effectively, assign tasks strategically, and resolve conflicts efficiently. Rather than seeing personality differences as obstacles, leaders should view them as assets that, when properly managed, contribute to a well-rounded team.

Developing Adaptability: The PI-Shaped and Polygon-Shaped Professional

While recognizing personality types is essential, it is equally important to cultivate versatility in employees. In today’s fast-changing work environment, specialists who only excel in one area may struggle to adapt. This is where the concepts of PI-shaped and Polygon-shaped professionals come into play.

Leaders should develop team members to be:

  • PI-Shaped Professionals: These individuals have deep expertise in two distinct areas while maintaining a broad understanding of multiple disciplines. This allows them to contribute meaningfully across various projects while still possessing specialized knowledge that sets them apart.
  • Polygon-Shaped Professionals: The most adaptable, these individuals possess multiple deep skills across various disciplines. They can seamlessly switch between different roles, making them invaluable assets in dynamic industries that require agility.

Steps to Develop Adaptability

  1. Encourage Cross-Training: Employees should be given opportunities to develop skills outside of their primary expertise. This could be done through mentorship programs, job rotations, or training workshops.
  2. Foster a Learning Culture: Organizations should invest in ongoing education, providing access to courses, certifications, and hands-on experiences that allow employees to expand their skill sets.
  3. Allow for Experimentation: Employees should be encouraged to step outside their comfort zones, take on new challenges, and learn from their failures. Creating an environment that rewards curiosity fosters long-term adaptability and resilience.

By developing PI-shaped and Polygon-shaped professionals, organizations build a workforce that can evolve with changing industry demands and navigate uncertainty with confidence.

Placing People in the Right Situations: Applying the Cynefin Framework

Even the most talented and well-trained individuals will struggle if placed in the wrong environments. The Cynefin Framework by Dave Snowden provides a structured approach to decision-making and problem-solving by categorizing work environments into five domains:

  • Simple (Clear & Ordered): Tasks in this domain are straightforward and require a structured, rule-based approach. Green and Blue personalities excel in these roles, as they thrive on consistency and precision..
  • Complicated (Expertise-Driven):Problems require analysis and expertise but have clear solutions. Blue and some Red personalities do well in this environment due to their logical and results-driven nature..
  • Complex (Emergent & Adaptive): Problems are unpredictable and require experimentation to find solutions. Yellow personalities and Polygon-shaped professionals are best suited for this space, as they are comfortable navigating ambiguity.
  • Chaotic (Crisis & Rapid Response): Immediate action is required, and there is no time for structured analysis. Reds are ideal for leading in these scenarios due to their decisiveness and confidence.
  • Disorder (Unclear Situation): Leaders must first identify which of the above domains applies before proceeding. Effective leadership in this domain requires flexibility and strong situational awareness.

By aligning people with the appropriate environment, leaders can prevent frustration, enhance productivity, and ensure that each team member operates at their highest potential.

Bringing It All Together: A Leadership Blueprint

Successful leadership is not just about understanding people, developing skills, or placing talent—it’s about integrating all three into a cohesive strategy. Leaders should:

  1. Understand Your Team: Use Erikson’s color model to recognize personality types.
  2. Develop Multi-Skilled Professionals: Encourage PI-shaped or Polygon-shaped growth to foster adaptability.
  3. Place Talent Strategically: Apply the Cynefin Framework to ensure individuals are working in environments where they can thrive.

By mastering these three dimensions—understanding people, fostering adaptability, and aligning talent with the right context—leaders can create high-performing, engaged, and resilient teams ready to tackle any challenge. The future of leadership is not about rigid hierarchies but about dynamic, people-focused strategies that maximize potential and drive meaningful success.
